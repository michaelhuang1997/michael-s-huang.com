The Development Machine: Pakistan (1947–1958)

Scope

Independence to the eve of Ayub Khan’s coup (Aug 1947–Oct 1958). Focus on donors, instruments, motivations, and key data points.

1. Background: A New State’s Constraints (1947–1953)

Partition left Pakistan with a narrow industrial base, severe refugee resettlement needs, and thin fiscal capacity. Early support came through the Colombo Plan (1951) from UK/Canada/Australia and via entry into the World Bank/IMF (1950). The World Bank’s first loan—$27.2m for Pakistan Railways in 1952—financed locomotives and track rehabilitation [1]. U.S. involvement started with Point Four technical assistance and 1953 emergency wheat shipments [2].

2. Alignment & Aid Surge (1954–1958)

After the 1954 Mutual Defense Assistance Agreement and Pakistan’s entry into SEATO (1954) and the Baghdad Pact/CENTO (1955), U.S. economic (grants/loans, PL-480) and military aid (tanks, aircraft, MAAG, training) rose sharply [3]. Late-1950s U.S. commitments are commonly summarized as ≈ $2.5bn economic and $0.7bn military (nominal) [4]. The UK provided Commonwealth training/technical assistance; Canada financed Warsak Dam (late-1950s) under the Colombo Plan [5]. The World Bank expanded lending—power (KESC), industry (Karnaphuli Paper), ports—and a second railways loan of $31m (1957), with cumulative 1952–1958 lending of $110m+ [6]. It also chaired Indus Waters negotiations from 1952, paving the way for the 1960 treaty [7].

3. Donors & Instruments

  • United States. Development grants/loans, PL-480 food, FX support; substantial military aid & training tied to alliance politics [3] [4].
  • United Kingdom & Commonwealth. Colombo Plan TA; officer training (e.g., Sandhurst); advisory roles; projects incl. Warsak [5].
  • World Bank (IBRD). Railways, power, port, industry lending; Indus Basin mediation and donor coordination groundwork [6] [7].
  • Other bilaterals. Australia/New Zealand/Canada experts & scholarships; West Germany joined coordination by late-1950s [5].

4. Motivations

Aid was embedded in Cold War strategy—Pakistan’s “Northern Tier” position, India rivalry, and base access shaped U.S./UK policy. Multilaterals emphasized productivity and infrastructure while facilitating dispute resolution [3]
