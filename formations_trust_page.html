Formations of Trust

Project Overview

This project documents the historical narratives and descriptions of major newspapers regarding foreign aid and its donors in Pakistan from 1971 to the present. Political scientists and economists who study foreign aid and accountability often assume that the recipient public is indifferent towards aid donors and attribute credit to donors as well as incumbent politicians if aid is seen as effective.

Research Questions

However, this assumption may not hold in practice. Through systematic analysis of Pakistani newspaper coverage, this project examines how media narratives shape public perceptions of foreign aid donors and challenges conventional assumptions about recipient public attitudes toward international assistance.

Methodology

The project employs computational text analysis methods to examine decades of newspaper coverage, tracking how different aid donors are portrayed in Pakistani media and how these narratives have evolved over time. This approach allows for a comprehensive understanding of the discursive landscape surrounding foreign aid in Pakistan.

Significance

By documenting these "formations of trust" in Pakistani media, this research contributes to our understanding of how recipient publics actually perceive and discuss foreign aid, providing crucial insights for scholars of international political economy, foreign aid effectiveness, and public opinion research.

Timeline

The project covers the period from 1971 to the present, allowing for analysis of how aid narratives have shifted across different political eras, international contexts, and donor relationships in Pakistan's history.
